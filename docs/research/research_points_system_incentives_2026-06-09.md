# Research: points-system incentive design and the win:draw ratio

**Date:** 2026-06-09
**Project:** wcpool (FIFA 2026 World Cup draft-pool scoring design)
**Topic owner:** points-system incentive design — choosing W:D for the group-stage scoring layer
**Status:** research input to the group-stage scoring extension (group W/D points + progressive knockout points). Decision doc not yet written.

---

## 1. Scope & question

The prior study ([recommendation_2026-06-08.md](../tables/recommendation_2026-06-08.md)) scored only the *terminal* knockout stage a drafted team reached (group exit = 0). We are extending the scheme to award **group-stage points for wins (W) and draws (D)** — no points for goals or losses — and **progressive points** through each knockout round. This document addresses one sub-question of that extension:

> How should the ratio **W:D** of points-for-a-win to points-for-a-draw be chosen for the group-stage layer, and what does the football-economics literature on league points systems (the 1990s 2-1-0 → 3-1-0 switch) imply for that choice?

**Critical framing — two distinct effects.** The football-economics literature studies the **player-incentive** channel: changing W:D changes how *teams choose to play* (offensive vs defensive effort, risk-taking, sabotage), which changes the realised distribution of match outcomes. **This channel does not exist in our simulator.** In wcpool, match outcomes are generated by a fixed strength model — independent-Poisson goals with a calibrated supremacy slope (Maher 1982 framework; [strength.py](../../src/wcpool/strength.py)), yielding Skellam W/D/L probabilities that depend only on the rating gap. No agent re-optimises play in response to our pool's payoff table. The literature is therefore relevant to us **only as a structural/design analogy**, not as a behavioural prediction. The decision-relevant channel for us is the **scoring-design effect**: how W:D reshapes the *spread, variance, granularity and sum-structure* of pool points across the 48 teams. Section 4 develops this; Section 2 establishes what the behavioural literature does and does not robustly show, so we do not over-claim from it.

**Independence from FIFA 3/1/0.** wcpool's `tournament.py` uses FIFA's 3/1/0 **internally** to decide group standings and who advances ([assumptions.md §1](../assumptions.md)). Our **pool** W:D is a *separate, free design choice*: the points a drafter earns for a team's group result need not equal the points the tournament uses to rank that team. We can mirror FIFA (3:1) or not (2:1, 1:0.5, 1:0) without touching advancement logic.

## 2. Key findings (claim + citation + evidence)

The behavioural literature on 3-1-0 is **genuinely mixed**: theory is ambiguous, and empirical results split between "fewer draws / more goals" and "no net effect / perverse effects." This matters for us mainly as a caution against assuming W:D has any clean, monotone effect on *outcomes* — but recall (Section 1) our outcomes are fixed regardless.

**F1 — Theory is ambiguous: 3-points-for-a-win can *lower* offensive incentives.**
Brocas & Carrillo (2004), a game-theoretic analysis, show that "contrary to the common belief, the incentives of teams to play offensively may be lower under the three-point victory than under the traditional two-point victory." The leading team's incentive to protect a winning position (now worth 3, not 2) can dominate the trailing team's incentive to chase an equaliser.
*Evidence:* peer-reviewed game theory, *Journal of Sports Economics*. [doi:10.1177/1527002503257207](https://doi.org/10.1177/1527002503257207) [VERIFIED].

**F2 — Largest empirical study: 3-1-0 *did* significantly raise goals and reduce draws.**
Moschini (2010), a structural model tested on "a large data set that spans 30 years and 35 countries," finds "the 3-point system has led to a statistically significant increase in the expected number of goals and a decrease in the fractions of drawn matches."
*Evidence:* peer-reviewed, *Economic Inquiry* — the broadest cross-country panel in this literature. [doi:10.1111/j.1465-7295.2008.00177.x](https://doi.org/10.1111/j.1465-7295.2008.00177.x) [VERIFIED].

**F3 — German Bundesliga diff-in-diff: draws fell, but the *leader plays more defensively*.**
Dilger & Geyer (2009), using a difference-in-differences design (league games as treatment, German Cup games as control, so the effect is attributable to the rule), find "the number of draws should decrease … the strategy of a leading team becomes more defensive, resulting in fewer goal shootings by that team … Empirical evidence supporting these effects is found." So fewer draws **co-exist** with *less* attacking by the team that's ahead — consistent with F1's mechanism.
*Evidence:* peer-reviewed, *Journal of Sports Economics*. [doi:10.1177/1527002508327521](https://doi.org/10.1177/1527002508327521) [VERIFIED].

**F4 — Spain La Liga: no net change in goals/draws, but more "sabotage" (dirty play).**
Garicano & Palacios-Huerta study the same incentive change with diff-in-diff on La Liga and find the policy "exerted no impact on either the number of goals scored or the number of drawn games but did induce a greater degree of sabotage" — teams raised both offensive and *destructive* effort (more fouls/cards; when ahead, more defenders, fewer goals, fewer opponent attempts), netting to no scoring change.
*Evidence:* CEPR Discussion Paper 5231 (2005), "Sabotage in Tournaments: Making the Beautiful Game a Bit Less Beautiful," and the peer-reviewed Princeton University Press monograph *Beautiful Game Theory*, ch. 8, [doi:10.23943/princeton/9780691144023.003.0009](https://doi.org/10.23943/princeton/9780691144023.003.0009) [VERIFIED as a book chapter / discussion paper]. **No standalone peer-reviewed journal article exists** — CrossRef returns only the discussion paper and the 2014 book chapter (the `gphjeea3.pdf` filename on the author's site implies a JEEA submission, which I could **not** confirm as a published JEEA article) [PARTIAL].

**F5 — Bundesliga regression-discontinuity: the rule "does not work as FIFA intended."**
Hon & Parinduri (2016), using a regression-discontinuity design, "do not find evidence that the three-point rule makes games more decisive, increases the number of goals, or decreases goal differences," with only weak evidence that losing first-half teams score marginally more in the second half.
*Evidence:* peer-reviewed, *Journal of Sports Economics*. [doi:10.1177/1527002514531790](https://doi.org/10.1177/1527002514531790) [VERIFIED].

**F6 — FIFA's / IFAB's official rationale: discourage defensive play, encourage attacking.**
The rule originated in England (Football League, 1981, proposed by Jimmy Hill) explicitly so "teams will not settle for a draw." FIFA first used it at the 1994 World Cup and formally adopted it in 1995. The FIFA Museum states the 1994 system was used "as a way of motivating teams to play a more attacking style" after Italia '90 "lacked the attacking flair of previous World Cups."
*Evidence:* official ([FIFA Museum, USA '94](https://www.fifamuseum.com/en/explore/fifamuseumplus/blog/USA-94-A-World-Cup-o)) [VERIFIED] + tertiary ([Wikipedia, Three points for a win](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_points_for_a_win)) [VERIFIED]. The stated *intent* is documented; whether it *worked* is exactly the contested question of F1–F5.

**Synthesis of F1–F6.** Across the literature the only claim with broad support is the *direction-of-incentive* on draws (the dominant strategy of *not settling for a draw* is weakly favoured), and even that is theoretically reversible (F1) and empirically null in two well-identified single-league studies (F4, F5). There is **no consensus effect size** transportable to a different competition. For wcpool this reinforces the framing: do **not** import any behavioural number; the literature's value is conceptual.

## 3. Quantitative values relevant to our design

**3a. The one descriptive datum often cited for the WC (use with care).** The first 3-1-0 World Cup (1994) averaged **141 goals / 52 matches = 2.71 g/match**; the immediately preceding 2-1-0 tournament (1990) averaged **115 / 52 = 2.21**, "a record low that still stands."
Sources: [1994 WC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1994_FIFA_World_Cup), [1990 WC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1990_FIFA_World_Cup) [VERIFIED]. **Caveat (do not treat as causal):** this is a single before/after pair with no control, confounded by squad/refereeing/tactical-era changes; the academic causal evidence (F1–F5) is the proper basis and is mixed. It is included only to characterise the magnitude people associate with the switch. It is **not** an input to any W:D recommendation.

**3b. The model's own draw rate (this *is* a design input).** wcpool's calibrated strength model implies an even-match draw probability within the band asserted in [test_strength.py](../../tests/test_strength.py) (0.20–0.35); point value **≈ 0.27** per [assumptions.md §2](../assumptions.md), within the historical World-Cup band. Across a 48-team field the per-match draw probability varies with the rating gap (lower for mismatches, ~0.27 at parity). This is the quantity that, combined with W:D, determines the *expected group-stage points* and their *spread* — see 3c.

**3c. Group-stage matches and the constant-sum threshold (definitional, no magic number).** Each of the 12 groups plays a single round-robin of **6 matches → 72 group matches total** ([assumptions.md §1](../assumptions.md)). Per match the points the **pool** distributes are:
- a decisive result (prob `1 − p_draw`) pays **W to one team, 0 to the other → total W**;
- a draw (prob `p_draw`) pays **D to each → total 2D**.

So the **expected pool points distributed by one match** is `(1 − p_draw)·W + p_draw·2D = W + p_draw·(2D − W)`. The bracket vanishes **iff `D = W/2`** (i.e. **2D = W**). This is the structurally important value: at **D = W/2 every match distributes exactly W regardless of outcome**, so the **total group-stage points are a fixed constant `72·W` in every simulation** — independent of how many matches were drawn. Above `D = W/2` (e.g. 3:1, since 2·1 > 3 is false — see table) the group-points total *rises* with the number of draws; below it (e.g. 1:0, wins-only) the total *falls* with more draws.

| Pool W:D | W | D | 2D vs W | Per-match pool total | Group-stage total across 72 matches |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| 3:1 (FIFA-mirror) | 3 | 1 | 2 < 3 | 3 (win) / 2 (draw) | `3·(#decisive) + 2·(#draws)` → **varies**, falls with draws |
| 2:1 | 2 | 1 | 2 = 2 | **2 (always)** | **144 exactly (constant)** |
| 1:0.5 | 1 | 0.5 | 1 = 1 | **1 (always)** | **72 exactly (constant)** |
| 1:0 (wins-only) | 1 | 0 | 0 < 1 | 1 (win) / 0 (draw) | `1·(#decisive)` → **varies**, falls with draws |

Note the FIFA 3:1 sits **below** the constant-sum line (2D = 2 < 3 = W): under 3:1 a drawn match pays out *less* in total (2) than a decisive one (3), so a simulation with many group draws distributes *fewer* total group points than a chalk-heavy one. The two ratios on the line — **2:1 and 1:0.5** — are the *same scoring rule up to a scale factor of 2* (1:0.5 = ½ × 2:1), so they are identical in every relative/ranking sense; 2:1 keeps integer points.

**3d. Granularity / spread per ratio (structural consequences, to be quantified in the sweep).**
- **Spread of group-stage points across teams** is governed by `W − D` (the gap between a win and a draw) relative to the scale. Normalising so a win pays the same nominal W, the *draw discount* `1 − D/W` is **2/3** for 3:1, **1/2** for 2:1 and 1:0.5, **1** for 1:0. Larger discount → group points more concentrated on teams that *win* (more between-team variance, more skill signal from the group stage); smaller discount → draws nearly as good as wins (group stage compresses toward "matches played," less discriminating).
- **Wins-only (1:0)** maximally rewards winning and zeroes out draw-heavy teams, **but** it makes the group-stage total *vary most* with the draw count (highest 2D-vs-W gap in magnitude) — it injects the most simulation-to-simulation noise into the *aggregate* purse while maximising *between-team* contrast.
- **D = W/2 (constant-sum)** removes aggregate-purse noise entirely: the group layer becomes a **fixed-budget reallocation** among the 8 drafters each simulation. Combined with the (already near-constant-sum) knockout layer, this pushes the **whole pool toward an exact constant-sum game across drafters**, which (i) makes drafters' scores **negatively correlated by construction** (one's gain is another's loss), tightening the *relative* standings and (ii) removes a source of cross-simulation variance in the winning score, which interacts with the tie-rate and slot-equity metrics the prior study tracks ([assumptions.md §7](../assumptions.md)).

These are *structural* statements derived from the payoff algebra; their *magnitude* on the project's actual metrics (skill correlation, tie rate, slot-win-prob spread, champion-dominance) must be measured by running the W:D options through the existing simulation grid. No numeric W:D is recommended here without that sweep — consistent with the project's zero-magic-number rule.

## 4. Direct implications for our W:D choice

1. **Treat W:D as a scoring-design knob, not an incentive lever.** Because match probabilities are exogenous in wcpool (Section 1), none of the behavioural results (F1–F5) predict any change in our *tournament outcomes* from changing W:D. What changes is purely how a *fixed* outcome distribution is *scored*. Frame and document the choice that way; do **not** justify a W:D by appeal to "encourages attacking play."

2. **The decision-relevant axis is the draw discount `1 − D/W` and the constant-sum line `D = W/2`.** Two qualitatively different regimes:
 - **`D = W/2` (2:1 ≡ 1:0.5):** group layer is **constant-sum** (fixed purse `72·W`), making the overall pool closer to an exact constant-sum game among drafters — cleanest *relative*-standings interpretation, lowest aggregate-purse variance, draw worth exactly half a win.
 - **`D < W/2` (3:1 with W=3,D=1; or 1:0):** draws are penalised harder; group points concentrate on winning teams (more group-stage skill signal) at the cost of an outcome-dependent total purse. **1:0** is the extreme — draws score nothing, maximal between-team contrast, maximal aggregate-purse noise.

3. **3:1 (FIFA-mirror) has one practical merit and one structural quirk.** Merit: it reuses the exact number the tournament already shows on the standings table, so a layperson reads "you get the same points the group table gives." Quirk: it sits *below* the constant-sum line, so unlike 2:1 it does **not** make the pool constant-sum and a draw-heavy simulation distributes a smaller group purse than a decisive one (3d). If the design goal is "pool standings are a clean zero-sum contest," 2:1 is structurally cleaner than 3:1 despite 3:1's familiarity.

4. **The group layer must be *commensurate* with the knockout ladder.** This W:D choice does not stand alone: group points are added to the progressive-knockout points. A team that wins all 3 group matches earns `3W` from the group layer; under each ladder that should be calibrated against reaching R32 (the first knockout payoff). The W:D *scale* (W=1 vs W=3) therefore interacts with the knockout ladder's first rung — this cross-layer balance is a separate design question flagged here as a dependency, not resolved in this scoping document.

5. **Recommended evaluation, not a recommended number.** Per the project's no-magic-number standard, run the four candidate W:D options {3:1, 2:1, 1:0.5, 1:0} through the existing resampled-draw simulation grid and report each on the four established metrics (skill correlation, tie rate, slot-win-prob spread, champion-dominance; [assumptions.md §7](../assumptions.md)), exactly as the ladder study did. The *a priori* structural expectation to test: constant-sum 2:1/1:0.5 should **reduce tie rate and winning-score variance** relative to the others (fixed purse), while 1:0 should **raise group-stage skill signal but also aggregate-purse variance**; 3:1 sits between, with the familiarity benefit. The empirical run adjudicates; this document supplies the hypotheses and the algebra they rest on.

## 5. Open questions / residual uncertainty

- **Cross-layer scale (W vs the ladder's first rung) is unresolved.** Whether W should be 1 or 3 (or other) depends on the desired weight of group-stage results against a single R32 appearance — needs joint specification with the knockout ladder, then a sweep. Not decided here.
- **Constant-sum interaction with existing metrics is predicted, not measured.** The claim that `D = W/2` lowers tie-rate / winning-score variance is a structural deduction; its size on wcpool's metrics is unquantified until the sweep runs.
- **Behavioural literature is internally contradictory (F2/F3 vs F4/F5) and not transportable** to a fixed-probability simulator anyway. We use it only conceptually; if a future version of the engine ever made play endogenous, this evidence base would need revisiting (and the contradictions resolved per setting).
- **Garicano & Palacios-Huerta provenance is partial.** The empirical "no goals/draws change, more sabotage" result is verified only at the discussion-paper + peer-reviewed-book-chapter level; I could not confirm a standalone JEEA journal article despite the suggestive filename. The claim's substance is well-attested across both forms; only the journal-of-record is uncertain.
- **The 2.71-vs-2.21 WC datum is descriptive only.** It is a single confounded before/after comparison and is explicitly *not* an input to any W:D number; flagged so a later reader does not mistakenly causal-ise it.
